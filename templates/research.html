project

    Asymmetry in Labour Dynamics between Canadian-born and Immigrant Groups

abstract

    This study investigates asymmetry in employment and wage rate convergence between individuals from different immigrant (socio-demographic) backgrounds to the Canadian-born population. We use the Longitudinal Immigration Database (IMDB), the Longitudinal Administrative Database (LAD), the Labour Force Survey (LFS), and the Census from 1986 to 2016. Through descriptive and regression analysis we identify the determinants of any disparities in employment and wage rate convergence over time since arrival. Our methodology involves comparing immigrant cohorts with the Canadian-born for the years of the quintennial Census. Controlling socio-economic factors, e.g., educational attainment, occupational experience, language ability and other demographic factors, we observe the presence of disparity in economic integration for different groups under identical labour market conditions. Moreover, we extend our analysis to examine whether there is an asymmetry in convergence across immigrants based on gender and region. Using the Blinder-Oaxaca wage decomposition framework, our findings ascertain whether differences in labour dynamics can be explained by immigrant's occupational human capital or characteristics unrelated to occupation, providing insight on discrimination. In addition, we use Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to derive an index for indivduals' skill level as a latent class variable and investigate skill transferability. Females from the Middle East and North Africa (MENA) have the lowest labour force participation rate globally while at the same time some of the highest levels of education in the developing world. Therefore females from MENA countries may stand to improve economic outcomes most from discussion on skill transferability and underutilization in Canada. Our findings provide evidence for policy development with the scope of eliminating labour market disparities for different immigrant groups.
